Quick Topic Review

Section 2: Fundamentals of Security
  • CIA Triad: confidentiality prevents unauthorized disclosure, integrity prevents unauthorized change, availability keeps systems reachable.
  • Non-repudiation uses digital signatures: hash the message, encrypt the hash with the sender's private key, verify with the sender's public key.
  • AAA order matters: authenticate first, authorize second, account/log after access.
  • Zero Trust: never trust, always verify. Policy Engine decides in the control plane; PEP executes in the data plane.
  • Risk exists only when a threat and a matching vulnerability are both present.
Section 3: Threat Actors
  • Threat actor is the who, threat vector is the how, attack surface is the where.
  • Nation-state/APT usually signals high sophistication, long-term persistence, custom tooling, espionage, or strategic goals.
  • Organized crime is money-driven, even when tooling is advanced or the group acts as a proxy.
  • Shadow IT is usually convenience-driven, not automatically malicious, but still creates unmanaged risk.
  • Honey family by scale: honeytoken, honeyfile, honeypot, honeynet.
Section 4: Physical Security
  • Bollards stop vehicles; fences define and delay perimeter intrusion.
  • Access control vestibules prevent tailgating and piggybacking by controlling one door at a time.
  • Piggybacking involves consent; tailgating does not.
  • Biometric sensitivity lowers FAR while raising FRR; CER is the balance point.
  • RFID and NFC badge cloning is a physical attack indicator on the exam.
Section 5: Social Engineering
  • Social engineering attacks the human decision process, not the firewall.
  • Pretexting is the story; impersonation is the false identity.
  • BEC uses a real compromised internal email account, which makes it harder to spot.
  • Misinformation is false without intent; disinformation is deliberate deception.
  • Security awareness training should be recurring and reinforced with simulations.
Section 6: Malware
  • Virus requires a host file and user action; worm spreads by itself.
  • Ransomware encrypts files and demands payment; isolate first and restore from known-good backups.
  • Rootkits hide at kernel level and require external boot scanning for reliable detection.
  • Logic bombs wait for a date, event, or condition before executing.
  • Impossible travel, missing logs, resource spikes, and out-of-cycle logging are compromise indicators.
Section 7: Data Protection
  • Data Owners classify data; Data Custodians manage the systems that protect it.
  • Data at rest, in transit, and in use each need different protection methods.
  • Tokenization replaces data with a vault-backed token; encryption is reversible with a key.
  • Data sovereignty follows the physical location of the data.
  • DLP location matters: endpoint catches activity on the device; network catches data leaving the perimeter.
